> "Any company still asking LeetCode in 2026 isn't worth joining." — Is that true?
>
> In the AI era, the *purpose* of interviews is shifting. Does hand-writing algorithms still mean anything? How much is still pure memorization? Which companies have moved to **real-world coding / system design / pairing with AI**? This repo aims to give the developer community a **transparent, crowdsourced** answer.

This is an **open, contribution-driven** list. Share your own interview experience — whether or not the company still asks LeetCode. The more data, the more the community benefits.

> ⚠️ **Disclaimer**: Everything here is crowdsourced and reflects individual candidates' experiences at a specific time for a specific role. It **does not represent any company's official policy, nor is it a judgment of any company or hiring advice**. Practices vary widely across teams, roles, and interviewers. Please use your own judgment, and corrections are welcome.

## 🤔 Why this repo?

"Still asks LeetCode" is often read as a signal that a company's engineering culture is **outdated**. But in 2026, that judgment got more complicated:

- **AI makes rote memorization meaningless** — an LLM writes the optimal solution in seconds, so testing "did you memorize it" is worth less and less.
- **But algorithmic thinking still matters** — many companies use LC as a **thinking filter**, not a memory test, and allow discussion and AI assistance.
- **Formats are diverging** — some moved to real-world coding, system design, AI pairing; others still hand-write the same problems.

Rather than argue about "worth it or not," let's **lay out the facts** and let everyone decide for themselves. This repo draws no conclusions — it just provides data.

For a deeper take on the trends, see [docs/trends.md](docs/trends.md).
